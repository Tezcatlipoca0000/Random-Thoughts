Homo homini lupus est (Wiki Link)
I don't believe economy, in general terms, is very fair to the majority. That's my postulate for this topic.
It's pretty easy to imagine things from a position of ignorance, and yes I don't know anything about economy, but I still feel convinced about my initial postulate; Let me rephrase it: "Capitalism is not very fair to the majority." - Not that I think that communism is any better.
What bugs me the most is that our economy system interferes with our self-preservation notions (which affects us in many ways). The majority of us is given only two options: either you work, pretty much like a slave, in terms of how many time you've to spend working and the benefits you obtain, or you starve, and nobody gives a crap about starving people, well mostly anybody.
Self-preservation is a strong force within us, capable of making us change, sometimes in ways we never imagined. Most people think they're in control of themselves, and while that's somewhat true (let's say 10% true) it's also oh so not true. Self-preservation is one of those forces within us that's capable of overriding most, if not all, of the ego's ordinances but it's not limited to the ego, every other force (e.g. sexuality, moral, intelligence, indoctrination, etc.) also has the potential of being affected by the all-mighty self-preservation force.
When our self-preservation kicks in, be it because we're in current danger or maybe because we're taking an important decision that will affect our capabilities to live better or worse for our near or distant future, when it kicks in, I mean, we become more animals and less humans. Almost anyone (very few people will put ethics above their interests less alone above their self-preservation), when faced with the choice of earning more money at the expense of doing something slightly unethical, most people will choose the unethical path because earning more money means, not only living better, but also, in part, securing our future, our self-preservation.
I'm just questioning the idea... Is competitiveness against our fellow species and individualism the correct path to produce resources? Is starvation really necessary for society to function correctly, is poverty needed, do we really have to distribute world's wealth like we're doing right now? I speak from what I see, so I might be totally wrong here, but I think there are more people that have to spend their lives working than people that can spend their lives living. I see the necessary people for a company to function correctly living 'barely' and a very few 'lucky' people that don't work living in opulence. Is threatening everybody with starvation the best way to make the humans produce? Maybe, just maybe, our economic system don't have the correct appreciation of the human soul; maybe enforcing production the way it's done is not as effective.
I'm not asking to get everything for nothing, I'm not talking about having your self-preservation means secured for free. I do believe in working your weight in society, I'm just questioning the amount needed to invest. Is the amount worked being paid fairly? Is wealth distributed fairly among society?
I'm sure things differ from country to country and even from city to city, but in a global scale, do workers have time for themselves? I mean to nourish themselves, not just to cover basic necessities, are people being educated to be something else than a good slave?
I believe a good understanding of the human "soul" is needed in order to create a 'fair' and 'productive' system. I also believe that a good investment on a human will yield good results; I don't think it's necessary to threaten everyone with starvation, exposure and discrimination for them to give something back to society, I believe everyone has 'something' that could turn to be productive, the thing is, why would anybody share what they have willingly in a competitive, individualistic and aggressive environment?

A first point, that should be evident, is that 'good' derives from the sovereign science, the most fundamental science of all; and it's precisely the political science. It is, in fact, the one that determines which are the sciences indispensable for the existence of the States, which are the ones citizens must learn, and until what degree they must possess them. Besides, it's important to observe, that the most cherished sciences are subordinated to the political; I mean, military science, administrative science and rhetoric. As it uses all practical sciences and prescribes also in the name of the law what must be done and what must be avoided, you might say, that it's goal includes the goal of every other science; and, thus, politics would be the true 'good', the supreme 'good' of man.(Aristotle. Nicomachean Ethics. Book 1. Chapter 1)
Ancient Greeks had so beautiful ideas of what politics and good governments should look like; today's governments around the globe, most of them anyways, are a real shame. It seem to me that maintaining the status quo has become the only main thing governments do this days; most of them have become experts fabricating news and molding people perceptions into making them more obedient; making virtuous citizens is the least of their concerns. In fact individualism and competition has created corruption in every level of the government system almost, if not so, worldwide. And having under-educated citizens makes it easier to making them subservient and compulsive.
Check this page out to see how widespread corruption is: ourworldindata.org
The democratic system as well is in need of revision. I feel confident that we, intellectual species, can do better than: "the majority rules", I mean reality is usually a little bit more complex than that, and to think that the majority is always correct sounds frankly stupid. The majority is not always correct, in fact many, many times the majority is wrong. And to make things worse we try to 'simplify' things and avoid giving each vote a different value, when it's obvious that, depending on the reason, each vote should 'weight' differently; for instance, if you know that this vote was casted the way it was because someone gave him some things (maybe a gift basket with a lot of products with ads) in exchange for that vote, you might be inclined to value it a little bit less than a vote casted that or the other way but with reasonable arguments, especially those that takes in consideration the benefit of the majority. Anyhow, I don't know much about politics either, I think it would be easy to imagine a system that's, at least, a little bit more fair to the majority; but what do I know, I'm sure it's not because of corruption and an opportunity to gain individual gain that keeps us in this system; it's probably for the best, right?

Education of a human should include: knowing yourself, knowing the past and knowing the cosmos. I believe that's a huge mistake many partake. We shouldn't lie about the shit we're in, not to anyone, and especially not to a kid. Pretending we're perfect just stagnates the human development, instead give the kid a good view of what's wrong and he'll probably try to help, and with enough time, he/she probably could. Know thyself (Wiki link)
To try to achieve a complete and comprehensible understanding of the human mind and capabilities would be an enormous task that would deviate from the purposes of this little pamphlet. All I want to remark here is: every human action has the intention of producing pleasure or alleviating pain, so every human action has, to say the least, a selfish component that's meant to pleasure. Don't matter what anyone says, even in virtue everyone's final goal is pleasure. (My own translation from: Cicerón, El bien y el mal supremos, I, 13, 42)
Because of that fact every human often lies to themselves and to others, to avoid recognizing hurtful truths they project to the exterior their own faults and believe the lies they tell themselves to make reality a little more bearable;
I had a teacher who often said The ego is a dirty corrupt, and I've learned to believe him. The human mind is not a single unity/entity/organization, it's more like clusters, like galaxies in the universe, you have really big, organized structures, and some very dispersed small constellations, or single objects flying about. And everything has its set of rules that govern every action, movement or phenomenon. Learning those set of rules is a key piece to understanding ourselves; knowing that a part of the ego, for the possibility of pleasure, can: forget things, make mistakes, change a memory and even change perception itself, and all this against another of the ego's part, is important. Being able to recognize our narcissism or a compulsive act can help us be more rational and commit less logical mistakes.
The human mind is complex, but like everything else in this universe, it's determined by laws. There's no psychological phenomenon that can exist without a reason. That only means there's really no free will; we are very little different from a rock. When I throw a rock this one can only land in one place, depending on certain variables in play (e.g. the force, weight, resistance, etc.), I might not know all the variables, and might not be able to calculate the exact spot where it will land but it still is determined; and the same goes for every psychological phenomenon, I mean, every thought, feeling, impulse, idea, or whatever, everything is determined by strict laws. Free will is an illusion; we 'feel' we're in control but we're actually just 'rolling downhill'. We're very similar to computers in some ways, some of the variables that determine us are 'inscribed in our hardware', I mean the body itself, and some other variables that affect the outcome are inscribed in our life experiences. New information can make the output somewhat different, depending on the information I feed the human mind. Among the variables that come given with the human body are those that make the subject more aggressive or more sexual, more or less intelligent, more or less clever, more or less perceptive, etc. are usually part of that which we refer to as the character and innate gifts/abilities. Regardless of the variables that come given in the human body, every ability, virtue or behavior can be taught, trained and developed if given the correct life experiences or information.

The knowledge about the past is as important as the knowledge about our nature, to know about the cosmos past gives perspective and, maybe-hopefully humility, to know the past of your species gives you another advantage that is a practical wisdom. It's been proven that information passes from generation to generation in the animal kingdom, but the gift of the written language has exploited that ability a thousand fold, being able to gain the cumulative knowledge of people who dedicated their lives to study a corner of the cosmos, for example, is an unprecedented advantage to a species; and that's without mentioning the, somewhat new, ability to send information at the speed of light (mind-blowing!). I'm confident the free access to information is key for humans to develop their potential. Therefore the knowledge about the past, in general terms, is an advantage to humans that shouldn't be underestimated.
Among the information I consider important about this subject would be: the information about the past of the cosmos, in general terms; the information about the past of the species, in general terms; information about early civilizations, early philosophers and early religions, in general terms; information about philosophy, in detail; information about civilizations and their conflicts of each period of history, in detail. It's important to try to understand our species through our past act, the heroic and the inhumane. Trying to hide the ugliness of our nature will not help us, quite the opposite.
